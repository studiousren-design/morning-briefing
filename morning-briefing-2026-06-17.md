## Section 4: Broader US Market — The Big Picture Today

Today is a **significant day for US markets**. Here is what you need to know.

### Federal Reserve Interest Rate Decision — 7:00 PM London Time
The Federal Open Market Committee will announce its interest rate decision at **2:00 PM Eastern Time — that's 7:00 PM here in London**. This is followed at **2:30 PM Eastern / 7:30 PM London** by a press conference from **Kevin Warsh**, who is serving as Federal Reserve Chair for the **first time** following his swearing-in on 22 May 2026, succeeding Jerome Powell.

The rate decision itself is almost certain to be a **hold** — markets are pricing roughly a **97% probability of no change**, leaving the Federal Funds target range at **3.50% to 3.75%**.

But the rate decision is almost beside the point. What markets are really watching is:

1. **The updated dot plot** — the Federal Reserve's projection of where policymakers expect rates to go. There is concern that the dots could shift in a **hawkish direction**, signalling fewer cuts ahead given that inflation is running at approximately **4.2%**, compounded recently by elevated energy prices tied to conflict in the Persian Gulf.
2. **Kevin Warsh's tone and communication style** — this is his debut as Chair, and investors will be studying every word for signals about how his Federal Reserve will behave differently from Jerome Powell's. The early read is that Warsh could be more hawkish, potentially shifting the Fed's bias from easing toward neutral or even tightening.

If Warsh comes across as hawkish tonight, expect pressure on growth and technology stocks, and potential support for the US dollar. If he strikes a balanced or dovish tone, markets could rally.

### US-Iran Ceasefire — A Positive Surprise
Earlier this week, markets surged globally following a **US-Iran ceasefire agreement** that ends the US blockade of Iran's oil and reopens the **Strait of Hormuz**. As a result, crude oil prices have fallen to around **$80 per barrel**, the lowest since mid-April. Lower energy prices ease inflation pressure and are generally supportive for equities, particularly consumer and industrial names.
